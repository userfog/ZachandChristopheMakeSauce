Abstract

Modernist thickeners are increasingly being used in the kitchens. We investigated the correlation between thickener concentration, temperature and viscosity of the most versatile and used thickeners - Xanthan Gum, Agar and Guar. We accumulated a lot of data, and then ran regressions to derive formulae that allowed us to make a website that tells you how much thickener to use for specified parameters - viscosity desired and temperature.

We would like to thanks Emily Redston, Sami Farhi, Helen Wu, Zach Gault, Pia Sorensen, Mary Wahl and Nathan Myrvold for their help and contribution to this project.

Motivation of Project

We cook, and love sauces. However, we do not like that traditional thickeners tend to require starches and fats, which are very calorie abundant and also alter the taste of the dish. We wanted to create a functional tool that would allow any chef to know exactly how much thickener to put. This is important given the potency of the thickers, as the range tends to be 0.05% - 2%. The specific motivation behind this was to thicken a red wine sauce.

Method

Range of concentrations used was taken from Nathan Myrvold’s chart on modernist thickeners. A scale to 3dp was used to measure samples at 0.05% increments. This was measure for sauce of 50ml. The samples were added to 50ml of beef broth, and then immersion blended until no more clumps. For agar, the broth was heated to 95 degrees before the addition of the thickener (it hydrates at 95 degrees). The resulting sauce was then topped up to 50ml, placed in a fridge to gel and then blended to become a sauce. Each sample was then tested in a temperature ramping rheometer with a 1 degree or 4 degree head with temperature ramp from 15 degrees to 60 degrees. Each test run took about 8 minutes.

Further Direction

Some areas to highlight. Since we measured the thickener in mass, and then added it to 50ml or 50g of beef broth, the resulting solution is greater than 50g. However, this difference is negligible as it is such a small percentage difference. Moreover, we would have liked to be able to measure temperatures above 60 degrees, but that was not possible within our timeframe.

Another limitation is that we used beef broth as representation for sauce. While this would work for red wine reductions, gravies and jus, it might not apply to non-water based sauces such as emulsions. This is simply the start. The dream would be for this to be a crowd-sourced database that would grow to incorporate more types of sauces.

Conclusion

We have succeeded a creating a tool that will be useful for us while cooking. Some areas for further work would of course we broadening this to other thickeners. We have covered the most important and versatile ones, but there are niche uses for the others and this function would be useful for some chefs.
